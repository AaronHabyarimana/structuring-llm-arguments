\chapter{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

This chapter evaluates the pipeline. Section~\ref{sec:evaluation:setup} describes
the experimental setup. Section~\ref{sec:evaluation:crosstopic} reports the
cross-topic results over the eight UKP topics (RQ1, RQ4).
Section~\ref{sec:evaluation:ablation} compares typed against binary prompting
(RQ2), and Section~\ref{sec:evaluation:gold} evaluates the attack quality against
a manual gold standard (RQ3). Section~\ref{sec:evaluation:case} works through a
small case study, and Section~\ref{sec:evaluation:errors} analyses the recurring
error types.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:evaluation:setup}

The pipeline is evaluated in two settings. The first starts from argument sets of
the UKP Sentential Argument Mining Corpus~\cite{stab2018}, which provides
sentence-level stance annotations for eight controversial topics. Each sentence
is labelled \texttt{Argument\_for}, \texttt{Argument\_against}, or
\texttt{NoArgument}. The \texttt{NoArgument} sentences are discarded and the test
splits are used. For each topic a balanced sample of pro and con arguments is
drawn with a fixed random seed for reproducibility. The second setting uses
LLM-generated arguments on the topic of allowing autonomous vehicles in public,
with ten pro and ten con arguments. Since the generation prompt already
constrains each argument to a single claim, atomization is skipped in that
setting.

In both settings the extractor and the verifier call the open-weight model
GPT-OSS-120B at temperature zero, with argument pairs processed in batches of
$b = 10$. Candidates are accepted at confidence $c \geq \theta_{\text{acc}} = 70$,
forwarded to the verifier at $50 = \theta_{\text{ver}} \leq c < 70$, and rejected
below $\theta_{\text{ver}}$. All runs are logged with their configuration, seed,
and per-pair decisions, so that every number reported here can be traced back to
a concrete run.

Two observations stand out. First, every framework contains cycles, a direct
consequence of the rebuttal symmetrization described in
Section~\ref{sec:foundations:typology}. Second, six of the eight topics admit
several preferred extensions, and the number of stable extensions matches the
number of preferred extensions in every case. Only Cloning and School Uniforms
reduce to a single extension. These multiple positions are not asserted by a free
generation. They are derived from the model's own pairwise attack judgments
through the fixed semantics, and each is conflict-free and admissible by
construction. The acceptance rate varies widely, from 5.0 percent for Marijuana
Legalization to 31.8 percent for Death Penalty, which reflects genuine
differences in how directly the sampled arguments engage each other.

This section measures whether the typed attack prompting of
Section~\ref{sec:method:extraction}, which the paper advertises as its central
contribution, actually produces different or more reliable attacks than plain
binary prompting. A second extraction arm runs the identical atomized argument
set $\mathcal{A}'$ through a binary prompt that asks only whether an attack
exists, without the attack type and without the Pollock definitions. The two
resulting attack sets are compared per topic by their Jaccard similarity, both on
the raw directed edges before symmetrization and on the final edges, and both
frameworks are solved to compare the numbers of grounded, preferred, and stable
extensions.

\section{Case Study: Nuclear Energy}
\label{sec:evaluation:case}

For greater comprehensibility a small nuclear-energy sample is analysed in
detail. Starting from six balanced UKP arguments, atomization yields eight atomic
arguments, three pro and five con. Only the sentence about meltdown statistics is
split, into three separate claims. Of the $\binom{8}{2} = 28$ pairs, 25 are
queried after skipping pairs of atoms that share a source argument. Six attacks
are accepted. Rebuttal symmetrization adds two reverse edges, so the framework
has eight directed attacks, four undermining and four rebuttals.

The graph centers on the argument that nuclear power merits support because it is
virtually CO\textsubscript{2}-free, which the con side attacks from several
directions. The symmetrized rebuttals make the framework cyclic, which suggests
that several preferred and stable sets might exist. In this case, however, the
grounded extension already terminates by accepting all five con arguments and
rejecting the entire pro side, so grounded, preferred, and stable coincide on a
single extension.

\section{Error Analysis}
\label{sec:evaluation:errors}

The case study surfaces two recurring limitations, each from a different stage of
the pipeline.

First, the attack-type classification can mislabel a premise-directed attack as a
rebuttal. Since rebuttals are symmetrized into mutual attacks while undermining
attacks stay directed, a false rebuttal label introduces a bidirectional
conflict and therefore an unjustified cycle. The meltdown-fatality claims, for
instance, attack the CO\textsubscript{2} argument as rebuttals, although they
question its safety premise rather than directly negating its support
conclusion. An undermining label would have been more suitable and would not have
added a reverse edge.

Second, atomization can produce atoms that, read in isolation, no longer carry a
clear stance and end up neither attacking nor attacked. The claim that two of
three meltdowns cause no deaths, for example, understates rather than opposes the
pro side on its own and therefore stays isolated in the graph.
